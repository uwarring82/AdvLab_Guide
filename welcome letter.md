# A Letter to Participants

**Advanced Laboratory Courses — FP-2 & FP-EDU**  
University of Freiburg, Institute of Physics

---

Dear colleagues,

You've done laboratories before. You followed protocols, recorded data, calculated uncertainties. This course is different.

In the advanced laboratory, you're not executing procedures — you're doing physics. Real physics. The kind where you make decisions, notice when something unexpected happens, and reason through problems that don't have answers in the back of a book.

That's harder. And it's more valuable. But it only works if the environment is right.

---

Here's the problem we're addressing: laboratories have power imbalances. Tutors assess students. Students want good grades. That creates pressure — pressure to perform, to guess what's expected, to hide confusion instead of voicing it.

The result is often a simulation of learning. Nodding along when you don't understand. Avoiding the interesting question because it might seem stupid. Playing it safe.

That's not physics. That's theatre.

We've designed this course to break that pattern — not by pretending the power imbalance doesn't exist, but by making it visible and accountable.

---

**The foundation: fairness.**

We call it the Safe Space, and it's always active. It means you cannot be assessed on criteria you didn't know. You cannot be blamed for things outside your control. Every decision affecting you has a name attached and a reason stated. If you make a mistake, there's a path to fix it.

These aren't aspirations. They're rules. They hold because the structure enforces them.

All criteria are published before you begin. Deadlines are clear. Late submissions have defined consequences — not arbitrary ones. If something goes wrong that isn't your fault, escalation pathways exist, and good-faith escalation cannot lead to disadvantage.

For tutors, the same accountability applies. They follow protocols. They meet deadlines. They explain their decisions.

The Safe Space isn't about comfort. It's about fairness. It's the ground we all stand on.

---

**The practice: honest discourse.**

We call it the Communication Space. During the learning phase — the three steps of entrance session, active laboratory work, and findings session — students lead. Tutors observe by default.

Tutors intervene only when necessary: for safety, for clear factual errors that would block learning (and only when they're confident), or for process feedback. When tutors speak, they state their reason. These interventions are logged.

The goal isn't consensus for its own sake. The goal is that when you reach a conclusion, it reflects genuine understanding — not deference, not guessing what the tutor wants.

You're allowed to disagree. You're allowed to be wrong. You're expected to say what you actually think.

That's how learning happens.

---

**The frontier: disciplined creativity.**

We call it the Exploration Space. Some questions don't have established answers. Some methods haven't been tried. Exploration is encouraged — but tethered.

The rule is simple: if you can say what would show you wrong, you're exploring. If you can't, you're drifting. We don't punish drifting — we mark it. The mark says: this is outside the envelope for now. Maybe a tether will be found. Maybe not. Either way, you know where you are.

Exploration produces candidates, not conclusions. Conclusions require deliberation.

---

**The structure: three steps, then assessment.**

Each experiment follows the same rhythm. First, the entrance session — you present the physics, we discuss. Then, active laboratory work — you measure, analyse, document in your lab notes. Finally, the findings session — you present what you found, with uncertainties, compared to expectations.

These three steps are your learning phase. Formative feedback throughout, no grades.

Assessment comes after: written reports for standard experiments, or short report plus seminar presentation for your final experiment. The criteria are published. The process is transparent.

---

So that's the design: a foundation of fairness, space for honest discourse, room to explore with discipline, and a clear structure that separates learning from assessment.

For students, this is your opportunity to engage with physics seriously — and to be taken seriously in return.

For tutors, this is your opportunity to accompany learners through something real — not to perform expertise, but to share it.

We've built this structure because trust matters. And trust is earned through structure, not promises.

Welcome to the advanced laboratory. Let's do physics.
